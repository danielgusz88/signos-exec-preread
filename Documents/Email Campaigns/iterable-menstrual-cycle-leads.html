Un-phased:
How your menstrual cycle affects blood sugar

One moment you can’t stop thinking about chocolate, the next you feel like you could take on a 10K (even if you don’t). Hormonal changes play a huge role in how we feel throughout our menstrual cycle.

And since our hormones also regulate blood sugar levels, it’s important to understand how our blood sugar may change throughout our cycle so we can work with it, not against it.

So here’s what
you should know.

Follicular Phase

Days 6–13

The follicular phase begins on day 1 of your period and typically lasts until ovulation (around day 14 of a 28-day cycle, though it can range from 10 to 22 days). While your period itself might leave you feeling a little wiped, this is often the phase where energy picks up, mood improves, and sleep gets better.

So what’s going on?

Estrogen is at its highest during this phase, which promotes insulin sensitivity.

Our gut is also processing food more slowly, which means blood sugar tends to stay lower after eating, and we’re generally less hungry overall compared to the second half of our cycle.1

When researchers tracked 100 days of CGM data alongside daily hormone testing, participants spent 68.5% of the day in a healthy blood sugar range during this phase, with fewer high blood sugar events overall.2

How to put it to the test

Test carbs + Lift weights:

Experiment with carbs:

This is the phase where your body handles carbs best, so it’s a great time to get curious about how both simple and complex carbs affect your glucose. Try a meal now and repeat the same meal during your luteal phase to see the difference.

Lift weights:

Your muscles are better at pulling glucose out of the bloodstream for fuel during this phase.3 That means you may see a more noticeable glucose-lowering effect from strength training. Resistance training during the follicular phase may also lead to greater gains in muscle strength and size compared to the same training during the luteal phase.4

Ovulatory Phase

Day 14–21

Ovulation is short (typically occurring 14–16 days before menstruation), so it often gets lumped in with the follicular phase in research. But it’s worth paying attention to because this is where the metabolic shift begins.

You probably feel great right now.

Energy is high, you may be more social, and workouts feel a little easier.5

However, glucose levels are gradually starting to increase during this phase as the body transitions toward the progesterone-dominant luteal phase.

However, since insulin is still needed for the ovulation process itself (it’s actually required for the follicle to rupture and release the egg), insulin sensitivity is still relatively high.6

How to put it to the test

Push your workouts:

Thinking about taking a HIIT or cycle class? Peak power output is highest during ovulation.7 High-intensity and sprint-style exercise burn through glucose fast, so if you’re curious about how a hard workout affects your blood sugar, this might be the phase to test it.

Luteal Phase

Day 21–28

After ovulation, estrogen drops and progesterone takes over. If you’ve ever felt like your body is playing by a completely different set of rules during the second half of your cycle, you’re not imagining it. Glucose metabolism is impaired during the luteal phase.8

In the same CGM study, participants spent 66.8% of the day at healthy blood sugar levels during this phase (compared to 68.5% in the follicular phase), with more time spent in higher glucose ranges.5 The highest median glucose levels often occur around day 24.5.

Experiencing more cravings?

It turns out that insulin resistance during this phase is all in our heads (or our brains, to be exact). Researchers found that brain insulin sensitivity actually decreases during the luteal phase, which prevents cells from absorbing glucose efficiently and triggers an increase in hunger.9

Researchers suggest this may be by design: during the follicular phase, our body needs to direct glucose to high-energy demands like growing endometrium, so sensitivity stays high. During the luteal phase, the priority shifts toward energy storage in case of pregnancy.10

How to put it to the test

Eat, move, hack, swap:

Eat fiber/protein before carbs:

This is a good phase for seeing how food order impacts your glucose response since insulin sensitivity is currently low.

Get moving:

Short bursts of muscle activity can help glucose enter cells. Play around with a 15–20 minute walk after meals versus before meals to see which one has the biggest impact.11

Try 1–2 tbsp of vinegar:

Consider consuming apple cider vinegar before or with your high-carb meal to see if it improves insulin sensitivity or reduces post-meal spikes.12

Swap sweets with magnesium-rich foods:

Maintaining adequate magnesium levels can optimize insulin sensitivity and stabilize glucose metabolism.13 When sugar cravings hit, eat more nuts, seeds, and (yes!) even dark chocolate.
